2 Problem Definition and Decision Framework
2.1 Client Decision Context and Statistical Translation
The client requested an evidence-based and decision-ready assessment of long run and seasonal temperature
change in Central England. The request focuses on three linked decisions:
• Whether recent decades show a sustained shift in seasonal mean, minimum, and maximum temperatures
relative to earlier baselines.
• Which seasons are changing most and whether the pattern is better described by trend, step change, or
changing variability.
• Which metrics and thresholds should be used for internal monitoring and communication.
2.2 Scope, Target Population, Time Window, and Primary Outcomes
The target population is the HadCET Central England seasonal series, representing the Midlands region.
The unit of analysis is year, with seasonal outcomes for winter, spring, summer, autumn, and annual. The
primary outcomes for this report are seasonal mean, seasonal minimum, and seasonal maximum temperature
in degrees Celsius. According to the archived dataset metadata, seasonal mean coverage starts in 1659 and
seasonal minimum and maximum coverage starts in 1878. For direct comparison across all three metrics, the
common analysis window is 1879 to 2024.
2.3 Estimands and Success Criteria for Decision Support
The primary estimand for level change is the seasonal difference between a recent 30-year period and an
earlier 30-year period, estimated separately for each season and each metric. The primary estimand for long
run change is the seasonal trend measured in degrees Celsius per decade over the common historical window.
Secondary estimands include changes in seasonal variability and shifts in the full seasonal distribution between
baseline periods. Evidence is considered decision-usable when direction, magnitude ranking, and uncertainty
statements are coherent across descriptive and model-based checks. For monitoring purposes, quantitative
action thresholds are defined as the 10th and 90th percentiles of each season-metric distribution over 1991 to
2020, with a four-level status classification described in Chapter 7.

3.2 Inclusion/exclusion Rules and Known Data Limitations
The analysis includes the three HadCET seasonal totals series for mean, minimum, and maximum temperature.
These files are included because they contain the year-indexed seasonal values needed to compare baseline
periods and assess long-run change. Seasonal ranked files are excluded because they are not the underlying
time series used in this report. No external datasets, covariates, or spatial joins are included.
Known limitations arise from the structure of the source data. The series represents Central England as a
regional aggregate, so it does not capture sub-regional variation within the Midlands. The seasonal fields
summarise conditions across three-month periods, so short-duration extremes are not directly observed.
Coverage is unequal across metrics because the mean series begins in 1659, whilst the minimum and maximum
series begin in 1878. In addition, the first winter value in each series is missing because winter includes the
previous December, which is unavailable for the first recorded year.
3.3 Pre-processing and Feature Construction
The pre-processing pipeline removed the introductory metadata lines that sit above the data table, dropped
the final non-data line, retained the six analytical fieldsyear, winter, spring, summer, autumn, and annual,
converted the year field to integer, converted the temperature fields to numeric, and exported the cleaned
series for downstream analysis. Additional variables were created only to support later comparison and
diagnostics, including indicators for the shared comparison window, baseline periods, record completeness,
and the structural first-year winter missing value.
Missing-data handling was straightforward. The only missing entries were the first winter value in each of
the three series, which arises from the source definition of winter as previous December plus January and
February. These values were retained as structural missing values, and no imputation was applied.
Potentially influential observations were screened within each metric-season series using two explicit rules.
The first rule calculated a robust MAD z-score and flagged observations with an absolute value greater than
3.5. The second rule calculated the interquartile range and flagged observations belowQ1 - 1.5 x IQR or
above Q3 + 1.5 x IQR . This screening flagged 30 observations across the three series, including unusually
cold winters in 1963, unusually warm summers in 1976, and unusually warm autumn values in 2006 and
2022 to 2023. These observations were retained rather than deleted. The flags are spread across different
metrics, seasons, and historical periods, and the wider quality checks found no duplicate years, no year gaps,
and no non-structural missingness that would indicate a recording or import problem. On that basis, the
flagged values were treated as plausible climatic extremes whose influence should be examined later through
sensitivity checks rather than removed at the cleaning stage.
After cleaning, all three series were retained for analysis. The mean series contains 366 yearly records from
1659 to 2024, whilst the minimum and maximum series each contain 147 yearly records from 1878 to 2024,
so cross-metric comparisons are carried out on their shared period rather than by excluding minimum or
maximum altogether. The dataset is otherwise complete, with no duplicate years, no year gaps, and no
non-structural missing values.

4.4 Summary of EDA Findings and Analytical Implications
The two main checks converge on three design requirements for the subsequent analysis. The window
comparison must be interpreted against the background of a continuously moving trend, since both baseline
periods sit on the upward portion of the same long-run trajectory rather than at stable plateaus. The analysis
must be conducted separately for each season and temperature type, given the systematic differences in
rate behaviour across these groupings. The supplementary appendix checks indicate that distributional and
variability changes exist, but they do not alter the main conclusion that the primary analytical burden should
fall on level change and warming rate, with baseline shift magnitude estimated formally in the modelling

5 Methodology
5.1 Model choice & Assumptions
The modelling strategy must answer two questions, the first one is the baseline contrast between 1961 to
1990 and 1991 to 2020. The second one is the long-run warming rate over the full historical record.
Annual temperatures tend to be dependent from one year to the next, so ordinary least squares may understate
uncertainty if the residuals are treated as fully independent. For that reason, generalised least squares with
an AR(1) error structure is taken as the initial working specification, with later tuning used to assess
whether a more complex dependence structure provides a materially better fit. Each of the 12 season-metric
combinations is fitted separately because the EDA showed that change differs systematically across seasons
and temperature types.
This design assumes that each baseline window is approximately centred around its own mean, that residual
dependence can be represented by a low-order autoregressive process, and that the innovation termηt is
approximately normal. For the long-run trend model, the additional requirement is that a single linear slope
over 1879 to 2024 be interpreted as an average rate across the full record rather than as a literal description
of every sub-period.
5.2 GLS AR(1): Baseline Shift Model
The shift model estimateshow much warmer the 1991 to 2020 period was compared with 1961 to
1990 for a given season and temperature type. For each of the 12 season-metric combinations, the
model is fitted separately to 60 observations, comprising 30 annual values from the historical window and 30
from the recent window. The formal specification is
tempt =β0 +β1·1[recentt] +εt, ε t =ρεt−1 +ηt, ηt∼N(0,σ2)
Each symbol has a direct interpretation:
6

===== PAGE 7 =====

• tempt is the observed seasonal temperature in yeart, the raw number in the dataset.
• β0 is the mean temperature in the historical window, thebaseline levelagainst which the recent
period is compared.
• The indicator 1[recentt] acts as a switch, equalling one for years 1991 to 2020 and zero for years 1961
to 1990.
• β1 is therefore the primary estimand, representinghow much higher the recent window mean is
compared with the historical window mean, in degrees Celsius.
• The residualεt is the difference between that year’s observed temperature and the average level for its
window, and is modelled as an AR(1) process rather than assumed to be independent across years.
• The parameterρmeasures how much this year’s “above or below average” carries over into next year.
If ρis positive, a year that is warmer than its window average makes the next year more likely to be
warmer than its window average too, so adjacent observations are dependent rather than independent.
• ηt is the white-noise innovation remaining after removing that dependence, assumed to follow a normal
distribution with mean zero and varianceσ2.
The four unknownsβ0, β1, ρ, andσ2 are estimated jointly using REML in R. The model is fitted 12 times
in total, once for each combination of four seasons and three temperature types (mean, minimum, and
maximum). Each fit uses an entirely separate set of 60 observations and produces its own estimates ofβ1, ρ,
and the associated confidence interval. Table 1 and Figure 3 collect all 12 results.
Summer minimum in Table 1 is used here as a worked example to illustrate how the model works:
tempt = 10.92 + 0.62·1[recentt] +εt, ˆρ= 0.195
The historical-window mean is 10.92degC and the estimated shift is 0.62degC, which means that the average
summer minimum temperature in 1991 to 2020 is about 0.62 degC higher than in 1961 to 1990. The
estimated ˆρof 0.195 indicates mild year-to-year dependence in the residual deviations, so adjacent summers
are somewhat related rather than fully independent. The 95% confidence interval [0.316, 0.924] lies entirely
above zero, which means that the recent-window increase remains clearly positive even after the AR(1)
correction has widened the uncertainty interval.
Table 1 and Figure 3 below point in the same direction across almost all season-metric combinations. Every
point estimate is positive, which indicates that the 1991 to 2020 window is warmer than 1961 to 1990 for
mean, minimum, and maximum temperature in every season. The clearest evidence appears in spring, where
all three confidence intervals lie comfortably above zero and the estimated shifts are also the largest in
magnitude. Summer and autumn show the same positive pattern, although with slightly smaller shifts than
spring. Winter is the least certain case. Its point estimates are still positive throughout, but the lower bounds
of all three intervals fall just below zero, so the evidence for a winter baseline shift is relatively weaker than
other seasons.

5.3 GLS AR(1): Long-Run Trend Model
The long-run trend model asks a different question from the baseline shift model. Instead of comparing two
fixed periods, it asks how much temperature tends to change when year increases by one.
tempt =β0 +β1·(t−¯t) +εt, ε t =ρεt−1 +ηt, ηt∼N(0,σ2)
Here, β0 is the fitted temperature level at the average year of the series.β1 is the annual slope, soβ1×10
tells us how much temperature is estimated to increase per decade. The term(t−¯t) means that the model
measures each year relative to the middle of the observed time period, because this leaves the fitted slope
unchanged, makes the intercept easier to interpret, and reduces collinearity between the intercept and slope.
The definitions ofεt, ρ, ηt, and σ2 are otherwise unchanged from the shift model. Each season-metric
combination is again fitted separately, now using the full 1879 to 2024 record, so the estimated slope is less
sensitive to the exact placement of the two baseline windows and makes use of substantially more historical
information.
Spring maximum in Table 2 is used here as a worked example:
tempt = 12.55 + 0.0114·(t−1951.5) +εt, ˆρ= 0.141
β0 = 12.55 is the fitted spring maximum temperature at the average year of the record, andβ1 = 0.0114
means that the fitted spring maximum temperature rises by about 0.0114 degrees Celsius when year increases
by one. Multiplying that annual slope by ten gives 0.114 degrees Celsius per decade, which is the value
reported in Table 2. The estimatedˆρof 0.141 is clearly positive, so year-to-year dependence is not negligible
in this series. The 95% confidence interval [0.07, 0.159] still remains entirely above zero, so the long-run
warming signal remains positive after the AR(1) correction.

5.4 Uncertainty Quantification and Decision Risk
Uncertainty is quantified with 95% confidence intervals, computed as estimate±1.96×SE from the fitted
GLS AR(1) models. In Table 1, each interval is attached to the baseline-shift estimandβ1. In Table 2, each
interval is attached to the long-run trend estimandβ1×10. These intervals describe sampling uncertainty
under the stated model assumptions for each season-metric combination.
These intervals do not capture every source of uncertainty. They do not include model misspecification risk,
such as non-linearity in long-run warming, nor unmodelled structural variance change across the record. For
decision use, this means interval statements should be read as uncertainty conditional on the current model
class. The practical implication is that winter baseline shifts remain weaker because their intervals are less
separated from zero, while spring, summer, and autumn show stronger directional stability.

5.6 Model Comparison and Dependence-Order Selection
5.6.1 Candidate Specifications
This section compares three dependence specifications under the same mean structure for each model family.
OLS is included as the baseline specification where residuals are treated as independent, so it provides the
simplest reference for fit and interval width. GLS AR(1) is included because the earlier dependence diagnostics
reported non-negligible year-to-year persistence in several season-metric series. GLS AR(2) is included as a
higher-order alternative to test whether a second lag materially improves fit once first-order dependence has
already been modelled. For the baseline shift model, the mean structure remains the historical versus recent
indicator. For the long-run trend model, the mean structure remains the centred linear year term.

7 Monitoring and Communication Approach
7.1 Monitoring Framework
Seasonal mean-based statistics are used as the primary monitoring metric. The robustness analysis in Section
6.2.2 showed that the influential years flagged in Section 3.3 do not materially alter the long-run conclusions,
so the standard seasonal series is appropriate for ongoing monitoring without special treatment for individual
outlier seasons. Extreme seasons are captured through the P10 and P90 threshold bands defined in Section
7.2.
Two baseline windows are used for briefing purposes, as specified in the client brief:
• 30-year (1991 to 2020)is the primary decision baseline, used for all formal threshold calculations
and decision statements.
• 10-year (2015 to 2024)serves as a short-horizon signal for detecting whether recent conditions have
accelerated beyond the 30-year reference.
Figure 2 provides the empirical basis for this pair of windows. Around 1990, the rolling 30-year warming rates
turn consistently and strongly positive across all four seasons, marking a broad acceleration that motivates
using 1991 to 2020 as the primary reference period. Around 2010, rates in several seasons begin to fall back
from their peak, indicating that the pace of warming has moderated somewhat in the most recent decade
whilst remaining positive. The 10-year window captures this post-peak phase, so comparing the two sets of
thresholds in Section 7.2 shows directly whether recent conditions have already shifted relative to the full
30-year reference.
7.2 Material-Change Thresholds
P10 and P90 are used as the reference band boundaries. Each marks the outer 10 per cent of the reference
distribution, so a season falling below P10 or above P90 is a genuinely uncommon event relative to the 1991
to 2020 baseline, occurring about once in ten seasons under stable conditions. This frequency is low enough
to carry a clear signal for planning purposes without generating alerts so rarely that the framework becomes
insensitive to gradual change.

8 Limitations, Validity, and Alternatives
8.1 Threats to Validity and Limitations
8.1.1 Internal Validity
Section 5.5 showed thatˆρwas generally around 0.1 to 0.2 across the fitted series. AR(1) therefore fits the
climatic intuition that adjacent years are not fully independent, but it does not follow that the effect of
ρis large enough to materially change every inference. At the same time, AR(1) captures only one-step
dependence and AR(2) extends this only slightly. Neither is sufficient to guarantee that more complex,
longer-range, or non-linear residual structure has been fully represented.
8.1.2 External Validity
The conclusions apply to the HadCET Central England seasonal series and to annual seasonal temperature
conditions at that regional scale. Their generalisability is limited in two ways. First, they do not directly
establish finer spatial variation within the Midlands. Second, the dataset compresses temperature information
into annual seasonal values and does not retain monthly resolution, so its temporal granularity is therefore
limited, which makes it more suitable for season-level analysis than for finer-grained temperature variation.
8.1.3 Construct Validity
The Chapter 7 monitoring framework is intentionally heuristic. The use ofP 10 and P 90 as thresholds,
together with the 30-year and 10-year reference windows, was chosen for interpretability and operational
usefulness in internal briefing rather than because these settings are the unique mathematical optimum.P 10
and P 90 are preferable toP 25 and P 75 for this purpose because they do not trigger as frequently, but they
still represent an empirical compromise rather than a formally optimal cut-off. The same applies to the two
reference windows. The 30-year window provides a longer baseline and the 10-year window provides a recent
signal, yet both involve judgement rather than a single objectively correct choice.
8.2 Failure Mode That Would Change the Conclusion
The most plausible failure mode is that the model assumptions do not match the true error structure closely
enough. Both Sections 5.2 and 5.3 assume that the innovation termηt is approximately normal and use
normal approximations to construct standard errors and 95% confidence intervals. If the true innovations
are more skewed, more heavy-tailed, or more prone to extreme values than this assumption allows, then the
interval estimates may misstate uncertainty. Section 6.1 suggests that the central residual distributions are
broadly acceptable in most season-metric combinations, but the winter tail still departs visibly from the
reference line. This means that the direction of warming may remain credible whilst the precision of some
interval statements, especially in winter, is less secure than the model formally implies.

8.3 Alternative Approaches and Conditions for Preference
A plausible alternative is a dynamic linear model, which allows both the underlying trend and the error
structure to evolve through time instead of imposing one fixed slope and one fixed dependence pattern over
the full record. Relative to the GLS long run trend model in Section 5.3, this approach is better suited to the
changing warming rates shown in Figure 2, and it would allow changes in the mean structure and residual
dependence to be handled within one time-series framework.
It was not chosen here because the report required a common set of results that could be compared directly
across seasons and temperature metrics, and the baseline-shift model together with the single long run
slope provided a clearer decision-ready summary for that purpose. A dynamic linear model would become
preferable if the main objective shifted from estimating recent contrasts and full-period average change to
tracking how the pace of warming and the dependence structure evolve through time.
